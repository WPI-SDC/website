About cubesats

Origin of the CubeSat

CubeSats are a standard, inexpensive way to explore outer space in low Earth orbit and beyond. They consist of one or more 10cm cubed volumes that hold basic systems and a specialized payload. The most basic “1U” CubeSat is a 10cm cube, and some of the largest are 10cm by 20cm by 30cm, or “6U” CubeSats. Their small size and standardized deployment system make CubeSats popular for universities and other organizations to educate students and try out new space technologies relatively inexpensively and quickly.

 

California Polytechnic State University and Stanford University first proposed standardized design specifications for CubeSats in 1999 and launched the first CubeSats in June, 2003. Since August, 2012, about 75 CubeSats have been launched. CubeSats are continuing to grow in popularity in the space industry, and it is time for WPI with its rich history of spaceflight to join the program.

The SDC was first created with the intention of developing the “WPI Standard” for CubeSats- a set of open source drawings, schematics, and resources for other universities and organizations to be able to develop their own basic CubeSat structures while avoiding the extravagant costs of pre-made systems. These organizations could then spend more time developing unique payloads rather than overcoming the initial hurdles of making the most basic systems needed to make a CubeSat function in space. This ambitious goal has evolved into something new- a stepping stone towards the WPI Standard- yet is no less zealous.

 

The SDC is now competing in the NASA Cube Quest Centennial Challenge, the ultimate goal of which is to earn a launch on NASA’s Orion spacecraft during its EM-1 mission in 2018. The challenge begins with four ground tournaments (GT-1 through GT-4) where at their conclusion approximately  five CubeSats will be selected for launch on Orion. The teams that make it to space will then compete in the Lunar Derby and/or Deep Space Derby for various cash prizes.

Teams may compete in any of the Ground Tournaments, but are required to compete in GT-4 in order to be eligible for launch on EM-1. Teams must also rank among the top fifive in GT-2 in order to qualify for Payload Safety Reviews, and therefore EM-1 launch. However, teams are not required to launch on Orion in order to compete for the In-Space prizes and may instead utilize a third-party launch.

In-Space Prizes

When the winning CubeSats are launched from Orion, they will be located near the moon. At that time they may either position themselves in lunar orbit, or continue on further into deep space away from Earth. These two challenges are called the Lunar Derby and Deep Space Derby, respectively. The SDC CubeSat is designed to compete in the Deep Space Derby, in which there are a number of objectives each team may pursue:

                •  Best Burst Data Rate

                •  Largest Aggregate Data Volume

                Sustained Over Time

                •  Spacecraft Longevity

                •  Farthest Communication Distance

                from Earth

Teams may attempt to win as many or as few prizes as they wish. A total of $1,500,000 in prize money is available to be distributed amongst teams that perform the best at each of the challenges..
